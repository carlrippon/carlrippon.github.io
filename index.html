Economics of disasters

About

A simple question, asked of islands.

I'm a PhD candidate in economics at the University of the Antilles, in the CREDDI research center. My work is on the economics of disasters in small, open, climate-exposed island economies, with a focus on the Caribbean.

It starts from a question that sounds simple and turns out not to be: why do similar shocks send comparable economies onto such different paths? To answer it, I combine structural macroeconomic modeling with applied empirical work, using DSGE models, local projections, and climate and satellite data.

I also care about what the results mean in practice: where reconstruction effort pays off, how price-regulation schemes are designed, and how islands can share the risk of shocks that, in a single event, can exceed a full year of output.

Working paper DSGE

One Mechanism, Three Islands: How Hurricanes Transmit Through Caribbean Small Open Economies

A two-sector DSGE model of Jamaica, Dominica and the Bahamas. Across very different islands, hurricanes transmit through the same hierarchy of channels, and output falls more from the lost efficiency of surviving capital than from physical destruction.
